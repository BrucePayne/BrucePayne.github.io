Payne College Football Power Ratings - Explanation

Composite Ratings

The Payne College Football Power Ratings use a composite of two different systems, a MOV or margin of victory system, and a WL or Win/Loss only system. The ratings in each system are computed independently, and then a weighted average is used to combine the two ratings. The systems are defined in more detail below. To predict a game based on the Power Ratings, the home team is predicted to win by its power rating plus the home field advantage minus the away team’s power rating (if the result is negative, then the away team is predicted to win by this margin). Click on any team name from the main ratings page, and you can see detailed predictions for that team for the regular season.

 

WL Ratings

The WL or Win/Loss ratings consider only the winner and loser of each game played. No consideration is given to margin of victory, home field advantage, or date the games are played. This system is completely BCS compliant and correlates well with the other computer ranking systems. To optimize the WL ratings the computer determines the probability that the victor of each game played matches the actual victor based on the current ratings. It will then iterate the ratings until a maximum total probability is reached for all games played to date. The probability function is based on the P-Win concept described in more detail below.

 

MOV Ratings

The MOV or margin of victory rankings use a predictive least squares fit of previous games played. The model used considers margin of victory (MOV), where the game played, and when the game was played. It includes all games involving at least one FBS school. 

 

By analyzing the score of each game played and minimizing the sum of the square of the errors of the expected scores for these games based on the power ratings, the computer iteratively adjusts the power ratings and the home field advantage until an optimal point is reached.

 

The MOV model keeps up with all FBS schools and all schools that play at least two games against FBS schools. The ratings from non-FBS schools are not included in the posted list however. These schools ratings only become meaningful to the FBS ratings when they play more than one FBS school, allowing for meaningful relative comparisons to occur.

 

To achieve a unique solution during the first four weeks of the season, the previous year’s games are considered very slightly. All of the previous season’s games are considered to occur on January 1st of the current year. The error squared term for each game is scaled by the following factor:

MAX(0, 1-D/270)

 

Where D is the number of days since the game was played. Thus a game played when the rakings are computed is scaled by a full 100%, but a game 270 days or more ago is not considered at all. Therefore all of the last year’s games are scaled down to zero after about the fourth week of the season. Even at the start of the season they are considered only a small amount relative to the current season’s games.

 

During the first few weeks this method can lead to some controversial results since it is weighed heavily on the relatively few games played to that point in the current season. However I don’t think it wise to weigh last year’s results very heavily. I’d rather not have to consider them it all, but something is required to develop a unique solution before all teams are “connected” to each other through games played. Keep in mind that many rating systems do not even attempt to rate the teams early in the season for this very reason. I believe it is still interesting to see how the teams rank even if it is less meaningful than it will be later in the season.

 

P-Win

The P-Win column in the rankings and prediction page is the probability that the team will win its next game. This assumes a normal distribution of result errors and a standard deviation of 15.72. This normal distribution matches well the college football data from the last several years. The “Visitor Win Prob” on the “All Predictions” page uses a similar assumption to compute probability. These probabilities are included in the probability model for the final record and the Prob Undef calculations defined below.

 

Final Records

Two models are used to predict a team’s final regular season record. The probability model uses the sum of the team’s chance of winning each game as its total wins for the regular season (and computes predicted losses similarly). The boolean model, just looks at each matchup and give the team a win for each that it is expected to win. The probability model is a better predictor of final record. The Prob Undef column is the computed probability that the team will finish the regular season undefeated.
